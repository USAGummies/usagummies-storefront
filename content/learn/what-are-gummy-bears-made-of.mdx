Gummy bears are one of America's most popular candies — over **$3.5 billion in gummy candy** is sold in the U.S. each year. But most people have no idea what's actually in them.

If you've ever flipped over a bag of gummies and squinted at the ingredient list, this guide is for you. We'll break down every ingredient in a typical gummy bear, explain what each one does, and show you what to look for when choosing a better option.

## The Basic Gummy Bear Formula

At their core, gummy bears are simple. The traditional recipe has been around since the 1920s when Hans Riegel invented them in Bonn, Germany. Every gummy bear starts with the same basic components:

### 1. Sugar

Sugar is the primary ingredient in most gummy bears, making up 40-50% of the final product. It provides sweetness and contributes to the gummy's texture — without enough sugar, gummies would be tough and rubbery.

Most brands use a combination of **sugar (sucrose)** and **corn syrup** or **glucose syrup.** The corn syrup isn't there for sweetness — it prevents the sugar from crystallizing, which keeps the gummy smooth and chewy instead of grainy.

### 2. Gelatin

Gelatin is what makes a gummy bear *gummy.* It's a protein derived from animal collagen (usually from pork or beef) that forms a gel when dissolved in warm water and cooled.

The gelatin concentration determines the texture:
- **Less gelatin** = softer, more melty gummy
- **More gelatin** = firmer, more chewy gummy

This is why different brands feel so different in your mouth. The quality and type of gelatin matters significantly. Higher-grade gelatin produces a smoother, cleaner chew.

**Note:** Because gelatin comes from animals, traditional gummy bears are not vegetarian or vegan. Some brands offer pectin-based alternatives, but the texture is noticeably different — more like a fruit snack than a classic gummy bear.

### 3. Water

Water is used to dissolve the sugar and gelatin during cooking. Most of it evaporates during the manufacturing process, but a small percentage remains in the finished gummy (typically 15-20% moisture content). This moisture level is what gives gummies their characteristic soft-but-firm texture.

### 4. Acids

Most gummy bears contain citric acid or a similar food acid. It serves two purposes:
- Adds a slight tartness that balances the sweetness
- Acts as a preservative that extends shelf life

You'll see **citric acid** (from citrus fruits), **lactic acid**, or **malic acid** (from apples) on most labels.

### 5. Flavoring

This is where gummy bears get their taste. Most commercial gummy bears use **artificial flavors** — lab-created compounds that mimic fruit flavors. These are cheap and consistent, which is why they're industry-standard.

Some brands use **natural flavors**, which are derived from real food sources. The FDA defines "natural flavor" as flavoring derived from a spice, fruit, vegetable, herb, or similar plant or animal source. ### 6. Colors — This Is Where It Gets Interesting

Here's where gummy bears diverge dramatically by brand. Color is the most controversial ingredient category in modern candy.

**Most mainstream brands use artificial dyes:**
- **Red 40** (Allura Red) — the most widely used food dye in America
- **Yellow 5** (Tartrazine) — linked to hyperactivity concerns in some studies
- **Yellow 6** (Sunset Yellow)
- **Blue 1** (Brilliant Blue)
- **Red 3** (Erythrosine) — banned by the FDA in January 2025

These synthetic dyes are petroleum-derived and have been the subject of ongoing debate about their effects on children's behavior and health.

**The alternative: natural colors.** A growing number of brands — including USA Gummies — use colors derived from fruits and vegetables:
- **Beet juice** or **black carrot extract** for red
- **Turmeric** or **safflower extract** for yellow
- **Spirulina** for blue/green
- **Paprika extract** for orange

These natural colors cost more and can be less vibrant, but they avoid the health questions surrounding synthetic dyes entirely.

## What About Coatings and Extras?

Many gummy bears have additional ingredients you might not expect:

- **Carnauba wax or beeswax** — gives gummies a slight shine and prevents them from sticking together in the bag
- **Coconut oil** — another anti-sticking agent
- **Mineral oil** — yes, some brands use this as a glazing agent
- **Starch** — used as a molding agent during production (the gummies are poured into starch molds)

These are generally in tiny amounts and are considered safe, but it's worth knowing they're there.

## How to Read a Gummy Bear Label

Next time you pick up a bag, here's what to look for:

**Check the ingredient order.** Ingredients are listed by weight. If corn syrup is before sugar, there's more corn syrup than sugar. If artificial colors appear, they'll be listed by name (Red 40, Blue 1, etc.).

**Look for "artificial flavors" vs. "natural flavors."** This tells you whether the taste comes from a lab or from actual food sources.

**Check for allergens.** Most gummy bears contain gelatin (animal-derived). Some may contain wheat, soy, or tree nuts depending on the facility.

**Look for the country of origin.** As we covered in our [Made in USA guide](/learn/made-in-usa-candy-why-it-matters), where your candy is manufactured affects the safety standards it's held to.
